## Where this started

The original concept was an EMR/EHR system with a built-in transcription model — essentially a voice-first clinical documentation tool. The sketch on paper included automatic speech recognition as a core feature, with the idea that doctors could dictate consultation notes rather than type them.

The system was always intended as a SaaS product hosted on the cloud, with hospitals and clinics onboarded as users under a multi-tenant architecture.

## The first major pivot

The ASR component was dropped early — not because it isn't a valuable idea, but because my supervisor [redacted].Fine-tuning Whisper on Nigerian medical speech required a labelled corpus of Nigerian clinical audio that doesn't exist in the public domain. Besides collecting that data would have taken longer than the project itself.

It's documented as future work.

## What replaced it

The removal of ASR forced a clearer question: what is the actual core value of this system? The answer became the data layer. Every EMR captures clinical data. Most Nigerian clinic EMRs — where they exist at all — just store it. This project's differentiation is what it does with that data once it's structured.

That reframe shifted the centre of gravity from input (how notes get into the system) to intelligence (what the system does with notes once they're in).

## The legal question about diagnostics

Early in the design, the AI feature was framed as a "diagnosis suggestion" system — the AI reads symptoms and suggests possible diagnoses. This raised a legitimate concern: in Nigeria, under the Medical and Dental Practitioners Act and MDCN guidelines, anything that could be construed as a system offering medical advice creates liability exposure.

The reframe was both a legal protection and a product improvement. Instead of the AI suggesting diagnoses, the system surfaces what the clinic's own doctors have previously diagnosed in similar presentations. The language shifted from:

<Callout type="definition" headings="definition" text="Possible diagnoses: Malaria (high likelihood)"/>

to:

<Callout type="definition" headings="definition" text="In similar presentations at this clinic over the last 6 months, the most common diagnoses were: Malaria (31 cases), Typhoid Fever (12 cases)."/>


Same information. Completely different legal category. The first is an AI making a medical claim. The second is a clinic's own historical data being organised and surfaced. The doctor still decides — the system just makes sure they're not deciding in an information vacuum.

## The intelligence dashboard as the real differentiator

Looking at comparable products — Vibrant Practice and August Health — clarified what "clinical intelligence" actually means in a well-designed system. It isn't just charts and tables. It's the system telling you what to pay attention to before you think to ask.

August Health's Insights section does this well: resident watchlists, rising risk flags, cross-community anomaly detection. That proactive framing — the system surfaces anomalies against the clinic's own historical baseline without being asked — became a design principle for the dashboard.

For a Nigerian primary care clinic, even basic aggregate analytics are transformative. Most boutique practices have no idea what their most common diagnoses are, which patients are overdue for follow-up, or whether a particular disease is presenting more frequently than usual. The intelligence dashboard makes that visible for the first time.

## Where the project landed

A cloud-based, multi-tenant EMR/EHR SaaS platform for Nigerian boutique and primary care clinics, with:

- Structured patient registration and consultation documentation
- AI-assisted clinical documentation support grounded in clinic historical data
- A real-time intelligence dashboard with proactive anomaly detection
- A natural language query interface for clinic-level and patient-level data
- Drug interaction checking against a curated Nigerian formulary
- Multi-tenant data isolation enforced at the database layer

The AI layer is not the product. The structured clinical data the system accumulates is the product. The AI is the interface to that data.
