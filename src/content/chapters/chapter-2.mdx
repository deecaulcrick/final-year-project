## 2.1 Introduction

The increased adoption of artificial intelligence in multiple industries has brought about massive changes in how we gather, process, analyse, and use information. Healthcare is one of the fields most affected by these changes, owing to the volume and complexity of medical data generated every day. In clinical practice, patient data is often recorded in the form of consultation notes, discharge summaries, laboratory test results, radiology reports, and prescriptions — most of which exist as unstructured text.

This informal structure is a significant obstacle to efficient use of data. Since these records cannot be represented in an organised manner, meaningful clinical observations cannot be extracted without a great deal of manual labour — a process that is time-consuming and prone to error. The problem is compounded in resource-constrained healthcare systems by insufficient health information infrastructure, inconsistent documentation practices, and a lack of interoperability between clinical systems. This is particularly acute in developing countries, including Nigeria.

Natural Language Processing (NLP) and Generative Artificial Intelligence have provided practical mechanisms to address this gap. Techniques such as Named Entity Recognition (NER), using domain-adapted models like BioBERT, can automatically identify and categorise clinical entities in unstructured text. Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) offers a hybrid approach to knowledge retrieval and response generation, enabling AI systems to produce responses grounded in actual medical documents rather than solely in learned parameters.

This chapter provides a systematic review of the literature underpinning this study. It examines AI in contemporary healthcare, outlines key NLP techniques for clinical text, discusses the foundations of generative AI and transformer-based language models, and critically evaluates RAG models and their applicability to medical record intelligence systems.


## 2.2 Artificial Intelligence in Healthcare

Artificial intelligence — broadly defined as the ability of computer systems to perform tasks that otherwise require human cognitive power, such as reasoning, learning, and decision-making — has gradually become a useful tool in clinical environments (Russell & Norvig, 2021). Clinical applications are wide-ranging and include diagnostic imaging analysis, predictive risk modelling, genomic interpretation, drug candidate screening, and clinical decision support.

The literature has well-documented AI's potential to enhance healthcare delivery. Topol (2019) argues that deep learning systems trained on large volumes of clinical data can match specialist performance on specific diagnostic problems — a finding supported by studies in radiology and pathology. AI-enabled solutions also offer the potential to reduce administrative burden by automating repetitive documentation, providing triage assistance, and generating clinical reports.

However, the distribution of AI in healthcare is uneven. In high-income settings, the widespread adoption of electronic health records (EHRs) has made structured patient data available at scale — the kind of training content needed to build robust AI models. In low-to-middle-income countries such as Nigeria, health records are often paper-based, digitised inconsistently, and not encoded using standard terminology. This disintegration restricts interoperability and makes AI systems difficult to deploy without substantial preprocessing infrastructure.

Further complexity arises from ethical considerations. Algorithmic bias, data privacy, and the context-specific sensitivity of medical decision-making all require careful management. AI models trained predominantly on Western clinical data may not generalise well to patients with different disease patterns, medication regimes, or documentation conventions — concerns that are particularly relevant in the Nigerian healthcare setting.

Despite these challenges, the literature confirms that AI, when applied carefully, has the potential to improve healthcare systems at all resource levels. The key is building systems that are flexible, interpretable, and grounded in the realities of the environments they are meant to serve.


## 2.3 Natural Language Processing (NLP)

### 2.3.1 Overview of NLP

Natural Language Processing is a field of artificial intelligence focused on enabling machines to read, manipulate, and produce human language in a way that is both syntactically and semantically correct (Jurafsky & Martin, 2020). Its applications span a wide range — from basic tokenisation and part-of-speech tagging, through to machine translation, summarisation, sentiment classification, and open-ended dialogue generation.

NLP draws on computational linguistics, statistics, and deep learning. Over the past decade, the field has undergone a significant paradigm shift, moving away from rule-based and statistical systems toward neural models — most notably transformer-based language models. These models learn rich linguistic representations from large corpora and can be fine-tuned for specific tasks using relatively small amounts of labelled data.

In the context of this study, NLP provides the foundational capability to process free-format medical text: detecting clinically significant entities, resolving ambiguities, and organising information in a way that can be intelligently retrieved and queried.

### 2.3.2 Clinical NLP

Clinical NLP applies natural language processing specifically to medical and biomedical text. Although the underlying computational techniques are similar to general NLP, clinical text presents a distinct set of challenges that make direct use of off-the-shelf models problematic.

First, clinical documentation is characterised by heavy use of abbreviations, acronyms, and shorthand that varies widely across institutions, specialties, and individual practitioners. For instance, "SOB" may denote shortness of breath, and "Dx" may denote diagnosis — conventions that do not appear in standard language corpora. Second, clinical text frequently contains spelling errors, incomplete sentences, and unstable grammatical structure, particularly in consultation notes written under time pressure. Third, medical terminology is highly specialised — including anatomical names of Latin origin, proprietary drug names, and disease classification codes — and largely absent from everyday language (Wang et al., 2018).

Addressing these challenges requires models trained on annotated medical corpora that reflect actual clinical practice, as well as preprocessing pipelines capable of normalising inconsistent input before linguistic analysis is applied. Significant progress in this area has produced a range of models and datasets explicitly designed for clinical NLP, several of which are discussed in Section 2.4.

## 2.4 Information Extraction in Healthcare

### 2.4.1 Named Entity Recognition (NER)

Named Entity Recognition is a structured prediction task in NLP that involves reading through a piece of text to identify spans of tokens representing a predefined set of semantically meaningful categories. In general-domain NLP, these categories typically include persons, organisations, locations, and temporal expressions. In the clinical field, the taxonomy is considerably more granular, encompassing medical conditions, symptoms, pharmaceutical agents, dosage values, laboratory results, anatomical references, and clinical events (Lee et al., 2020).

The value of NER in healthcare lies in its ability to transform the informal structure of free-text clinical records into a formal, machine-readable format. Extracted entities can then be mapped to standardised terminologies — such as the Unified Medical Language System (UMLS), SNOMED CT, or RxNorm — enabling interoperability and downstream computation. In the context of this study, NER forms the analytical core of the system: patient documents are read, annotated with clinical entities, and indexed for retrieval.

Contemporary NER systems are predominantly sequence-labelling models, most commonly based on transformer architectures trained on annotated corpora such as the i2b2 datasets or the BC5CDR corpus. These models assign a label to each token, typically using the BIO (Beginning, Inside, Outside) tagging scheme, and are evaluated on precision, recall, and F1 score per entity type.

### 2.4.2 Biomedical Language Models

The capabilities of clinical NLP systems have been substantially advanced by the development of pre-trained language models. Rather than training models from scratch on small, task-specific datasets, transfer learning allows a pre-trained model to be fine-tuned on a smaller clinical dataset — achieving strong performance with significantly fewer annotated examples.

A landmark development in this area is BioBERT, introduced by Lee et al. (2020). BioBERT is built on the BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations of Transformers) architecture and was pre-trained on large biomedical text corpora including PubMed abstracts and PubMed Central full-text articles. This domain-specific training gives the model a vocabulary and contextual understanding that is well suited to clinical language. BioBERT demonstrated substantially better performance than its general-domain predecessor across a range of benchmark tasks, including NER, relation extraction, and question answering.

ClinicalBERT extends this methodology by pre-training on de-identified clinical notes from the MIMIC-III database, making it particularly sensitive to the informal and idiosyncratic writing style common in bedside documentation. The open-source availability of both models has significantly lowered the barrier to developing high-performance clinical NLP systems, even in the absence of proprietary training data.

For the purposes of this study, a BioBERT-based NER pipeline is used to detect and categorise medical entities in uploaded patient documents. Its domain-specific pre-training makes it considerably more reliable in this task than general-purpose alternatives.


## 2.5 Generative Artificial Intelligence

Generative artificial intelligence refers to a class of computational models that produce new output — textual, visual, or audio — based on learned statistical regularities in training data (Goodfellow et al., 2014). Unlike discriminative models, which learn boundaries between predefined classes, generative models approximate the underlying distribution of real data, enabling them to produce plausible new examples.

The capability to generate coherent, contextually appropriate text at scale emerged with the development of deep learning architectures capable of modelling high-dimensional data. These systems have demonstrated remarkable fluency in natural language — generating coherent paragraphs, contextually appropriate dialogue, and domain-relevant summaries at a level previously achievable only by human authors.

In healthcare, generative models have been applied to automatic clinical note summarisation, patient discharge letter drafting, radiology report generation, and symptom-based patient query responses. However, a well-documented limitation of standalone generative models is that they frequently produce confident but factually incorrect responses — a phenomenon known as hallucination — which is especially dangerous in high-stakes medical contexts. This limitation is the principal motivation for adopting retrieval-augmented approaches, discussed in Section 2.7.

## 2.6 Core Architectures in Generative AI

### 2.6.1 Transformers and Large Language Models

Vaswani et al. (2017) introduced the transformer architecture in their foundational paper *Attention Is All You Need*, which fundamentally reshaped the NLP landscape. The central component of the transformer is the self-attention mechanism, which enables the model to dynamically weight the importance of each token within a sequence when encoding any given token — capturing long-range dependencies that earlier recurrent architectures could not efficiently model.

Unlike Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), which process tokens sequentially, transformers process entire sequences in parallel. This architectural property makes it possible to train at unprecedented scale, giving rise to Large Language Models (LLMs) with billions of parameters trained on corpora containing hundreds of billions of tokens.

Models such as LLaMA, GPT-4, and Falcon have demonstrated emergent capabilities across a wide range of tasks — including multi-step reasoning, code generation, and medical question answering — often without task-specific fine-tuning. Their contextual sensitivity makes them well suited to the generative role in RAG pipelines, where they are required to synthesise retrieved document fragments into coherent, contextually relevant responses.

### 2.6.2 Other Generative Architectures

In addition to transformers, several other generative architectures are worth noting. Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), introduced by Kingma and Welling (2013), learn compressed latent representations of input data and can decode these representations into new outputs, making them suitable for generating structured data. Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), introduced by Goodfellow et al. (2014), use an adversarial training process in which a generator network produces outputs designed to fool a discriminator network. GANs have achieved their greatest success in image synthesis.

More recently, diffusion models — trained to progressively denoise random signals into organised information — have established the state of the art in photorealistic image generation. While each of these architectures is significant within its respective domain, none is directly relevant to the text-based medical document intelligence system that is the subject of this work. For document understanding and natural language generation, transformer-based LLMs remain the most applicable generative architecture.


## 2.7 Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)

### 2.7.1 Overview and Motivation

Retrieval-Augmented Generation is a hybrid AI approach that integrates information retrieval with neural language generation to produce contextually relevant and factually grounded responses (Lewis et al., 2020). The core motivation is that standalone language models, regardless of scale, are inherently constrained by the knowledge encoded in their parameters at training time — knowledge that may be incomplete, outdated, or insufficiently domain-specific.

A RAG system addresses this by pairing a generative model with a dynamic retrieval system. Before generating a response, the system accesses an external document store, retrieves the most relevant segments, and makes these available as contextual input to the language model. The generator then produces its response based on both the user query and the retrieved evidence — substantially reducing the probability of hallucination and improving factual accuracy.

This architecture is particularly well suited to healthcare applications, where documents are highly domain-specific, frequently updated, and too varied to be practically encoded within a single model's parameters. A RAG-based medical record system can be extended simply by updating the document store, without retraining the underlying language model.

### 2.7.2 Architecture of RAG Systems

A complete RAG pipeline consists of five major components:

- **Document processing:** Raw medical data — clinical notes, prescriptions, laboratory reports — is ingested, split into semantically coherent units, and normalised.
- **Embedding:** Each document chunk is passed through an embedding model, which produces a dense numerical representation capturing the semantic content of the text in a high-dimensional space.
- **Vector database:** Embeddings are stored in a specialised index structure — typically a vector database — that enables efficient approximate nearest-neighbour search.
- **Retriever:** When a user submits a query, the query is embedded and compared against stored document vectors. The retriever returns the top-K most semantically similar chunks, which form the context window for the generator.
- **Generator:** A large language model receives both the user query and the retrieved context and produces a natural language response grounded in the available evidence.

### 2.7.3 Advantages of RAG in Healthcare Contexts

The literature identifies several advantages of RAG architectures that are especially relevant to medical record intelligence systems.

The most critical advantage in a clinical setting is reduced hallucination. Because the generator's output is conditioned on retrieved document segments with source attributions, the space for confabulation is substantially narrowed relative to purely generative models. This is essential in contexts where system outputs may inform clinical decisions.

RAG also offers domain adaptability without model fine-tuning. The system's knowledge base can be tailored to local clinical conventions, formulary lists, or individual patient histories simply by populating the document store with institution-specific or patient-specific records, while using a general-purpose backbone model.

The retrieval step improves transparency and explainability, as the system can attribute responses to specific source passages. This audit trail is important for clinical governance and for building trust among healthcare practitioners.

Finally, RAG systems are easier to keep current. The document store can be updated by adding or modifying records — in contrast to static LLMs, which require full retraining to incorporate new information. This is practically significant in the fast-moving field of medical knowledge.

## 2.8 Vector Databases and Embeddings

Reliable storage and retrieval of vector embeddings is central to the practical implementation of RAG systems. An embedding is a fixed-length numerical vector that encodes the semantic content of a piece of text in a continuous, high-dimensional space — typically between 384 and 1,536 dimensions, depending on the underlying model architecture.

The key property of well-trained embeddings is that semantically similar text segments are assigned geometrically similar representations, despite surface-level lexical differences. This allows the retrieval system to return contextually relevant document fragments in response to a query even when the exact words do not match — a major advantage over traditional keyword-based search.

Embeddings are most effectively stored and queried using purpose-built vector databases. FAISS, developed by Johnson et al. (2019) at Meta AI Research, enables approximate nearest-neighbour search across millions of vectors in milliseconds and is highly optimised for this task. Other platforms — including Pinecone, Chroma, and Weaviate — offer managed services with additional capabilities such as metadata filtering, hybrid search combining vector and keyword retrieval, and cloud-native scalability.

For medical record intelligence systems, the choice of embedding model should account for domain vocabulary. General-purpose embedding models from the Sentence-Transformers family perform well on standard text but may underperform on technical medical terminology. Production clinical applications should consider domain-specific embeddings based on biomedical pre-trained models, depending on the specificity of the use case.


## 2.9 Applications of RAG in Healthcare

The literature discusses RAG-based systems across several healthcare application areas. One of the most actively studied is clinical decision support, in which RAG pipelines retrieve relevant clinical guidelines, drug interaction data, or diagnostic criteria in response to practitioner queries. Such systems offer a transparent alternative to black-box classification models, as recommendations are grounded in authoritative source documents.

Medical document summarisation is another significant application. Rather than relying solely on a model's parametric memory, a RAG approach retrieves the most contextually relevant passages from a long clinical document before generating a summary — improving both relevance and factual accuracy.

Patient record querying — the primary application setting of this study — allows clinicians and patients to submit natural language questions about a specific medical document and receive evidence-based responses. For instance, a clinician might ask "what drugs was this patient prescribed on discharge?" and receive a direct answer drawn from the discharge summary, with the source passage surfaced for verification.

Primary care practices have also experimented with automated medical assistants powered by RAG, which support appointment triage, pre-consultation data collection, and symptom explanation. In both settings, the retrieval mechanism keeps responses grounded in actual clinical material rather than generalised model output.


## 2.10 Challenges and Limitations

Despite the substantial potential of AI-based medical record systems documented in the literature, several significant challenges must be acknowledged in any implementation context.

The most fundamental is data privacy and security. Medical records are among the most sensitive categories of personal data, and any effort to process them using AI systems — particularly cloud-based language models — raises complex questions around consent, data sovereignty, and regulatory compliance. In the Nigerian context, healthcare data handlers are subject to obligations under the National Health Act and emerging data protection frameworks, which must be reflected in system design decisions concerning where data is stored and transmitted.

Discrimination and bias represent a related technical and ethical concern. Language models trained on English-language, Western clinical corpora may not generalise fairly to patients from other demographic groups, language communities, or disease epidemiologies. Nigeria, with over 500 spoken languages and a highly diverse clinical documentation culture, presents an explicit risk of biased or unfair model behaviour that must be addressed in the evaluation design.

Although greatly reduced by RAG architectures, hallucination is not fully eliminated. Even when retrieved chunks are incomplete, inaccurate, or poorly matched to a query, the generator may still produce plausible-sounding but incorrect responses. This highlights the importance of high-quality retrieval, careful document curation, and systems that expose retrieved source material to human inspection.

Computational resource requirements present a practical constraint. The inference costs of large language models — in processing time and hardware expenditure — can be a barrier to real-time deployment in settings with unreliable power supply or limited internet connectivity. Smaller, quantised, or edge-deployed model variants may be required depending on local infrastructure.


## 2.11 Summary of Literature

This chapter has reviewed the literature most directly relevant to the development of an AI-based medical record intelligence system. It began by surveying the role of artificial intelligence in healthcare, and how machine learning and NLP have become increasingly central to addressing the longstanding challenge of unstructured clinical data — a challenge that is particularly acute in developing-country settings characterised by limited infrastructure and fragmented documentation practices.

The chapter then examined clinical NLP in depth, with particular emphasis on Named Entity Recognition and domain-specific language models — most notably BioBERT — that have advanced the state of the art in biomedical text processing. The transformer architecture underlying these models was explained, including the self-attention mechanism that enables the creation of large language models capable of sophisticated language understanding and generation.

The key technical paradigm of this study — Retrieval-Augmented Generation — was introduced, its architectural components described, and its advantages over purely generative approaches outlined. The infrastructure supporting RAG, including vector embeddings and vector databases, was examined alongside a survey of existing clinical applications.

A review of the challenges facing AI-based medical record systems — data privacy, algorithmic bias, hallucination risk, and computational constraints — completed the analysis, situating the technical opportunity within real-world constraints.

Taken together, the literature reveals a clear and substantive gap: while RAG and clinical NLP methods have demonstrated strong results in well-resourced environments using structured EHR data, they have not been studied in the context of fragmented, unstructured medical records within the Nigerian healthcare system. This gap is the driving force behind the proposed system in this work — a system designed to convert raw clinical documents into an intelligent, queryable source of knowledge accessible to both patients and practitioners.
